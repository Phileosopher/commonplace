
# What religion is

Religion is a type of positive mindset toward [the Unknown](unknown.md). Instead of raw [fear](mind-feelings-fear.md), a religious person will instead devote hope toward the unknown instead.

## Definition

Most discussions about religion need to start with the definition of the word. Like "[culture](people-culture.md)", religion can mean several vastly different things:

1. Devotion to something within the Unknown
2. A set of [rituals](habits.md) devoted to changing the Unknown toward a positive outcome
3. The culture surrounding those rituals

In particular, there are two groups of religious people who don't like to call themselves religious, both of them adhering to definition #2 stated above:

1. Atheists, who assume they have no positive mindset toward the unknown. Most of them on closer inspection, however, are either [futurists](https://gainedin.site/machines/) or [substance abusers](addiction.md).
2. Protestant Christians, who assert their relationship with Jesus doesn't have any religious elements.

Either way, the culture surrounding their values certainly betrays a steadier devotion than they themselves would like to admit.

## Classes of religion

The framing of a religion is based on the form of deity that we're assuming to exist in the Unknown.

- Agnosticism is the simple non-knowledge of a deity.
- Atheism is the absence of a deity altogether as a known fact. It often pairs with agnosticism, but creates more [certainty](understanding-certainty.md) on the matter.
- Monotheism has one God or, at the very least, one superior God above all other gods.
- Polytheism has more than one god with distinctive power over various domains.
- Pantheism vaguely defines God as a spirit or cosmic energy, which typically frames the logic that *everything* is part of God.

There are other mix-and-match forms of the idea (e.g., Panentheism), but there really isn't anywhere logical to go beyond those ideas.

Each of them has problems as well:

- Agnosticism is noncommittal, meaning that if there *were* any requirements for moving into the next life, there's a high chance of failure.
- Atheism has to contend with [the philosophical implications of no God](god-proof.md).
- Monotheism has to explain how an all-powerful God can permit a [sin-filled](theology-sins.md) world.
- Polytheism can only maintain itself if the gods themselves are the custodians of [virtue](morality-virtues.md), which makes their dualistic counterpart inferior, and enough reasoning means there can only be one god.
- Pantheism makes all human actions meaningless, since nothing is added or removed from the God that is the cosmic energy surrounding and within us.

## The social movement of religion

Many times, the culture of a religion outpaces the religion's precepts. Most critics of a religion will point to those cultural idiosyncrasies as if they are core to the religion, but they often aren't.

However, this situation isn't so straightforward. Many times, a cultural observance that wasn't officially part of a religion will be officially adopted as a canon part of the religion's observance (e.g., many parts of the Roman Catholic Church).

A religion becomes a [cult](people-culture-cults.md) when the members are unwilling to accept components of [reality](reality.md)

- This is a tricky thing, though, since the religion's value system frames a metaphysical structure that should be judged on the individual's actions in light of that structure.
  - Buddhism, for example, would allow someone to feel perfectly fine committing suicide. It would, however, also discourage someone trying to *convince* someone else to commit suicide. This means a suicidal person would be acceptable and assisted suicide might not be.

In one sense, all opposing religions of a religion are effectively cults.

APPLICATION: Don't be quick to call a legitimate religion a cult.
